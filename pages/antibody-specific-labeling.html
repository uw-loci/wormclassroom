Antibodies (Ab)

Antibodies are proteins produced by the immune system that recognize proteins from foreign organisms (infections, etc.) and tell the immune system to attack the infection. To make antibodies to a specific protein, the purified protein is injected into animals (often rabbits or mice) and they produce antibodies that can be collected in the blood of the injected animal. The serum is then purified to remove non-specific antibodies that donâ€™t bind to the injected protein. These purified antibodies can be used for a variety of techniques including immunofluorescence.

Ab-specific Immunofluorescence Labeling

To observe one's favorite protein (e.g. a protein called tubulin, the protein that makes up microtubules) in a C. elegans embryo, one can inject tubulin into animals (such as rabbits or mice) to produce anti-tubulin antibodies. With anti-tubulin antibodies, the amount and localization of tubulin can be detected in C. elegans embryos.
